A B S T R A C T

Phase-field finite element (PFFE) modeling of the quenching-partitioning-tempering (Q-P-T) process is proposed, and the two-dimensional PFFE-QPT model considering carbide precipitation and the interface migration between martensite and austenite is used to investigate microstructural evolution and the elastic/plastic strain distribution at quenching, partitioning and tempering stages in a high-carbon steel, respectively. The simulation results of the high carbon QP-T steel indicate that the precipitation strengthening of carbides occurs not only because they can block the movement of dislocations, but also because they can produce high internal stress. Meanwhile, the volume fractions of different phases (including primary martensite, retained austenite, secondary martensite, and carbide) and the carbon content in retained austenite predicted by the PFFE-QPT model are slightly better than those predicted by the novel one-dimensional QPT-LE (local equilibrium) model and much closer to experimental values. The PFFE-QPT model is also used to successfully predict the volume fractions of different phases in low-carbon and medium-carbon Q-P-T steels. More importantly, the microstructural morphologies closely related to mechanical properties can be demonstrated by the PFFE-QPT model and are comparable with the experimental observation. Therefore, the PFFE-QPT model will be a more powerful tool for guiding the process and microstructure design of Q-P-T steels compared with the QPT-LE model.

1. Introduction

The quenching and partitioning process (Q&P), proposed by Speer et al. (2003), is a novel approach for producing advanced high-strength steels at low cost. Different from the traditional heat treatment process, the Q&P process consists of (1) a quenching step at the temperature (Tq) ,which is between the martensitic start temperature (Ms) and the martensitic final temperature (Mf) to form a certain amount of carbon-supersaturated martensite and untransformed austenite; (2) the following partitioning step at the same temperature (one-step) or above (two-step) to transfer carbon from supersaturated martensite to untransformed austenite so that considerable carbon-enriched austenite can maintain its stability during the final quenching step to room temperature. After the Q&P process, the strength and the plasticity of the steel have been significantly improved (Seo et al., 2016a; Speer et al., 2005, 2011). Hsu et al. (Hsu and Xu, 2007) proposed a quenching-partitioning-tempering (Q-P-T) process, which serves as a modified Q&P process by absorbing the core idea of carbon partitioning, therefore, the Q-P-T steels also have considerable carbon-enriched retained austenite at room temperature. The Q-P-T process emphasizes the strengthening effect of carbide precipitation, which is not allowed in the constrained carbon equilibrium (CCE) thermodynamic model for the Q&P process (Speer et al., 2003). The addition of microalloying elements such as Nb can effectively promote the precipitation of carbides and grain refinement to make Q-P-T steels exhibit a better combination of strength and ductility (Zhang et al., 2015).

The mechanical properties of both Q&P (Hou et al., 2022; Wang and Huang, 2020; Toji et al., 2014, 2015) and Q-P-T (Yang et al., 2023; Zhang et al.,2011) steels are mainly tuned through the optimization of retained austenite fraction (VRA) and its carbon content (Cγ) ,although their compositions and processes are different to some extent. Combining the CCE model and the Koistinen-Marburger equation,the quenching temperature (Tq) can be predicted to obtain the maximum amount of retained austenite in Q&P steels (Thomas and Speer, 2014). However, the retained austenite fraction and its carbon content measured by experiments are always much lower than the predicted values (Seo et al., 2016b; Zhong et al., 2006). The discrepancies resulting from the migration of the martensite/austenite interface and carbide precipitation are not considered in the CCE model (Zhang et al., 2021).
Recently, Dai et al. (2018a, 2018b) proposed a quenching and partitioning-local equilibrium (QP-LE) thermo-kinetic model that considers the migration of martensite/austenite interface and carbon partitioning during the Q&P process. Therefore, the retained austenite fraction (VRA) and its carbon content (Cγ) predicted by the QP-LE model were in agreement with the experimental results for a series of low-carbon Q&P steels. However, in the QP-LE model, carbide precipitation was not considered, which could be acceptable for the low-carbon Q&P steel since carbon consumption by carbide should be insignificant. However, in the high-carbon Q-P-T steel, plenty of carbides will precipitate in the martensitic matrix for precipitation strengthening. Therefore, the effects of carbide precipitation on the interface migration of martensite/austenite and the partitioning of carbon must be considered in the high-carbon Q-P-T steels (Zhang et al., 2021). In view of this fact, a QPT-LE model is very recently proposed by us in the investigation of a Fe-0.67C-1.48Mn-1.53Si-0.038Nb (wt.%) high carbon Q-P-T steel (Zhang et al., 2021). The QPT-LE model can better predict the experimental results including the volume fractions of different phases and the Cγ compared with the popular CCE and QP-LE models (Dai et al.,2018a). Moreover,the universality of the QPT-LE thermo-kinetic model is verified by further prediction of VRA or Cγ in low-carbon and medium-carbon Q-P-T steels, being better than those predicted by the CCE model or QP-LE model (Zhang et al., 2022).
However, the QPT-LE model is one-dimensional and cannot be used to exhibit the microstructural morphology and evolution closely related to mechanical properties (Gao et al., 2013; Wang et al., 2019). The phase field model is a two- or three-dimensional one and can be used to simulate the evolution of microstructure, hence it overcomes the shortcomings of the QPT-LE model. There are currently numerous phase field models for the Q&P process (Ali et al., 2020; Toloui and Militzer, 2018). The migration of the martensite/austenite interface and the partitioning of carbon are considered by these models. The evolution of martensite and austenite and the redistribution of carbon with partitioning time can also be well exhibited. However, the models referring to carbide precipitation from the martensitic matrix during the Q-P-T process have yet to be reported. Therefore, these models will affect the correctness of the prediction of retained austenite fraction (VRA) .
In this work, phase-field finite element (PFFE) modeling of the quenching-partitioning-tempering (Q-P-T) process is proposed in the investigation of a high-carbon steel. In the PFFE-QPT model, the Cahn-Hilliard and Allen-Cahn carbide equations are coupled into the Q&P phase field model through the construction of the modified parameter to predict the carbide precipitation and the interface migration of martensite/austenite in the tempering process. The two-dimensional PFFE-QPT model is used to investigate the impact of carbide precipitation on the martensite/austenite interface migration and carbon partitioning in different stages of the Q-P-T process. Meanwhile,the evolution of microstructural morphologies is demonstrated. Then the characterization of transmission electron microscope (TEM), electron backscattering diffraction (EBSD), and three-dimensional atom probe tomography (3D-APT) will be carried out. The prediction accuracy of the PFFE-QPT model on the volume fractions of primary martensite, secondary martensite, retained austenite, transition carbide, and carbon content in retained austenite will be compared with the QPT-LE model and the experimental results. Moreover, the PFFE-QPT model will also be used to predict microstructural fractions and the carbon content in retained austenite in low-carbon and medium-carbon Q-P-T steels.

3. Model formulation

In this phase field modeling of the Q-P-T process, the micro-elasticity phase field model of the martensitic transformation is used to simulate the microstructure evolution in the quenching stage (Chen, 2002; Wang and Khachaturyan, 1997). The Kim-Kim-Suzuki model is introduced to simulate the diffusion of carbon from martensite to untransformed austenite in the partitioning stage following the quenching stage. Finally, the governing equations of the tempering stage are derived under the phase field framework to predict the redistribution of carbon and the evolution of the stress field with carbide precipitation. The evolution of the stress field is favorable for the analysis of mechanical properties and fracture mode in our subsequent study.

3.1. Phase field modeling for the quenching stage

The microstructural evolution in the quenching stage is described by using a technique, which is comparable to that in the literature (Schoof et al., 2018; Yeddu et al., 2017). Under isothermal conditions, the Gibbs free energy of martensitic transformation in the steel varies from the face-centered cubic (fcc) phase to the body-centered cubic (bcc) phase for energy minimization. Since this process does not involve diffusion, the Allen-Cahn equation is used to describe this diffusion-free martensitic transformation process. Eq. (1) determines the evolution of the order parameter over time by minimizing the change of the system’s total energy GQ with respect to the order parameter ηm(r→,t) ,which represents the degree to the phase transformation:
(1)∂ηm(r,t)∂t=−LQδGQδηm(r,t)+ξηm(r,t)
where δGQδηm(r,t) is the thermodynamic driving force in time and space, ηm(r,t) is the order parameter which tracks the evolution of martensitic, LQ is the kinetic coefficient, ξηm(r,t) is the Langevin noise term, GQ is the total Gibbs free energy of the system and can be expressed as:
(2)GQ=∫V(GQch +GQgr+GQel)dV
where GQch represents the chemical fraction of the Gibbs energy density of the system in the stress-free state at the specific temperature, GQgr represents the gradient energy density, GQel represents the elastic strain energy density.

3.4. Model and simulation conditions

In this study, the phase field model is used to predict the evolution of the microstructure and the distribution of carbon over the entire Q-P-T process. During the quenching process, the martensitic transformation is a shear transformation, the evolution of martensite microstructure and stress field is simulated by the Allen-Cahn equation of martensitic order parameter. The Kim-Kim-Suzuki model is introduced to the partitioning process and coupled with the Allen-Cahn equation of martensite to describe the carbon redistribution in martensite and austenite with the martensite/austenite interface migration. Since the carbide formation is a diffusional transformation, the Allen-Cahn equation to predict the evolution of carbide order parameters and the Cahn-Hilliard equation to describe carbon content are introduced in the tempering process. Then the governing equation of carbon concentration will change from the Kim-Kim-Suzuki model to the Cahn-Hilliard equation of carbides in this stage. The distribution of carbides in martensite and the impact of carbide formation on the microstructure of martensite and austenite are described by running the Allen-Cahn equation for martensite at the same time. Moreover, we can find that the carbides form in the martensite phase instead of the austenite phase during the Q-P-T process in experimental results. In order to ensure that the carbides are precipitated from the martensite region but not from the austenite region, the martensite order parameter is used as the initial condition to locate the martensite region which can be written as:
(25)Ψ(ηm)={0 for ηm<12−ep∗e5ϕep∗e5ϕep∗+e−5ϕep∗ for 12−ep∗≤ηm≤12+ep∗1 for ηm>12+ep∗
where Ψ(ηm) is a function associated with the martensite order parameter, ϕ is written as: ϕ=ηm−(12−ep∗),ep∗ is the fictitious value chosen so as to ensure the width of the region with martensite order parameter in (12−ep∗,12+ep∗) is similar to the martensite interface thickness and can be set to δ2 (Yeddu et al.,2012b).
It is worth noting that when the carbide precipitates from the martensite matrix, the order parameters of carbide and martensite will both be 1, which produces a “blind area” of uncertainty. To solve the “blind area”, the coupling effect is considered in Eqs. (3) and (22) at the carbide precipitation region where the martensite order parameter is reduced from 1 to 0 to indicate that this region is no longer martensite phase but carbides.

5. Discussion

5.1. Martensite/austenite interface migration mechanism during the Q-P-T Process

As well known, the driving force of the martensite/austenite interface migration caused by martensitic transformation is the Gibbs free energy difference (ΔGγ−α) of the system. The martensitic transformation can occur or not occur depending on whether the driving force can overcome energy barriers caused by the gradient (interfacial) energy and elastic strain energy. As shown in Fig. 5, the difference of the Gibbs free energy between the martensite and austenite phases (ΔGqr−d) drives the martensite/austenite interface migration during the quenching process (Van Bohemen et al.,2003). The green line (tq=4.5s) and purple line (tq=5.0s) overlap in Fig. 5,indicating that the Gibbs free energy difference (ΔGγ−α) and the energy barriers (induced by interfacial energy and elastic strain energy.) reach a dynamic equilibrium during the quenching process. Carbon partitions from the martensite side to the austenite side during the partitioning process as shown in Fig. 4. Due to the significantly faster diffusion rate of carbon in martensite compared to that in the austenite phase, the free energy density of the martensite phase significant changes, while the free energy density of the austenite phase remains essentially unchanged as shown in Fig. 5. The difference of the Gibbs free energy between the martensite and austenite phases in the partitioning process (ΔGp1γ−d) is lower than that in the quenching process (ΔGqγ−d) ,which cannot drive the migration of the martensite/austenite. However, due to carbon enrichment at the martensite/austenite interface during the partitioning process (as shown in Figs. 4d and 6b),the Gibbs free energy difference ΔGp2y−a is much higher than ΔGqy−a . The Gibbs free energy difference (ΔGp2y−a) gradually increases as the carbon content in martensite decreases from 0.67wt.% to 0.38wt.% (as shown in Fig. 6b),resulting in the balance between the Gibbs free energy difference and the energy barriers to be broken. The martensite/austenite interface migrates from the martensite side to the austenite side until a new dynamic equilibrium is established between the Gibbs free energy difference and the energy barriers (Speer et al., 2005, 2011). As the carbon concentration in the austenite phase gradually becomes uniform (as shown in Fig. 10b) during the tempering process, the free energy of the martensite phase becomes significantly higher than that of the austenite phase. However, the precipitation of carbides can change the stress distribution inside martensite and promote the migration of the martensite/austenite interface. Therefore,despite the further decrease of the carbon content in martensite (from 0.38wt.% to 0.03 wt. % as shown in Figs. 9b and 10b) caused by carbide precipitation, the martensite/austenite migrates slightly, and the movement distance (about 5nm as shown in Fig. 10a) is much smaller than that observed (about 112nm as shown in Fig. 6a) during the partitioning process (Zhang et al., 2021).

5.2. Precipitation strengthening mechanism induced by carbides

The evolution of the equivalent stress field during the quenching, partitioning, and tempering process in the high-carbon steel can be simulated by the PFFE-QPT model. Fig. 8a shows the equivalent stress distribution along the martensite/austenite interface after the Q-P-T process. Stress concentration near the martensite/austenite interface occurs after martensitic transformation, which is consistent with the equivalent stress distribution as shown in Figs. 3d and 4d . Meanwhile,the equivalent stress in martensite is higher than that in austenite as shown in Fig. 8a. This indicates that the mechanical properties of the steel have been significantly improved (Seo et al., 2016a; Speer et al., 2005,2011) after the Q&P process, and is attributed to the formation of martensite, which can change the stress distribution in the steel. As the tempering process proceeds, carbides gradually precipitate from the martensite region, resulting in a further increase of the equivalent stress as shown in Figs. 7d and 8b . The equivalent stress gradually increases from 1.72GPa (in martensite) to 4.39GPa (in carbides) during the tempering process. Moreover,the slight martensite/austenite interface migration induced by carbide precipitation (as shown in Fig. 10a), leading to the formation of martensite, can produce higher internal stress. This indicates that the precipitation strengthening effect of carbide arises from their ability to promote the martensite/austenite interface migration and produce high internal stress within the high-carbon steel (Hsu et al., 2013).

6. Conclusions

A phase-field finite element modeling of the quenching-partitioning-tempering (Q-P-T) process is proposed that considers multi-interface migration of martensite/carbide and martensite/austenite interfaces. This two-dimensional PFFE-QPT model is used to investigate the microstructural evolution at quenching, partitioning, and tempering stages for a high-carbon steel, respectively. As a result, the following important conclusions are obtained:
(a) The interface migration between martensite and austenite is towards the austenite side at the partitioning stage, leading to the gradual increase of martensite volume fraction and the decrease of austenite volume fraction.
(b) The interface migration between carbide and martensite is towards the martensite side during the tempering stage, leading to the gradual increase of carbide and the decrease of martensite. The formation of flake-like η -carbide in the martensite is attributed to the accumulation of carbon at the local area within the martensite phase. The redistribution of carbon in the martensite, induced by the precipitation of carbides during the tempering stage, leads to a reduction in the migration distance of the martensite/austenite interface, which is much less than that observed during the partitioning stage.

(A4)cm=(ca′,Fe+kC2a′xCa′+∑i⁡kia′xia′)[1+βa′(T−Tq)]
(A5)βα′=αα′,Fe+∑i⁡Kiαxiα

austenite (martensite). xiγ(xia) is the molar fraction of alloy element i in austenite (martensite). kiγ(kia) is the contribution of alloying element i to the lattice parameter of austenite (martensite). kC1d and kC2d are the contribution of carbon to the lattice parameter and cm , respectively.
